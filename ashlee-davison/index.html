About Ashlee

Ashlee Davison grew up in Maple Ridge, BC, attending Fraser Valley Christian High School before being recruited to Trinity Western University in Langley — part of the Spartans' very first CIS women's soccer recruiting class. She went on to become the program's all-time leading scorer, helped TWU win their first-ever CIS national championship in 2005, and then represented Canada on the Women's National Ultimate Frisbee team at the 2012 World Championships in Japan.

Today she is an athletics coordinator and teacher at École Kelowna Secondary School (KSS), a co-organizer of Okanagan Youth Ultimate, and one of the most respected youth sports coaches in BC's Interior.

Soccer Career

Davison played forward for the TWU Spartans from 2001 to 2006, wearing #9 throughout her career. She was one of three players in the program's inaugural CIS recruiting class, alongside Angela Trauter and Alexis Heinz. By graduation she had become the all-time leading scorer in the young program's history.

Her defining moment came in the 2004–05 season: 12 goals, a national championship, and the first major title in Trinity Western University's history. Head coach Graham Roxburgh — who would go on to win four more national titles — called that group the foundation of everything that followed.

After university, Davison moved to Kelowna and joined Royal Star in Division 1 of the Kelowna Women's Soccer League, where she scored 12 goals in just 7 games as her team went 12–0.

"I was blessed to be recruited, along with Angela Trauter and Alexis Heinz, to TWU in our first year of CIS women's soccer."

Team Canada — Ultimate Frisbee

Davison represented Canada on the Women's National Ultimate Frisbee team at the 2012 WFDF World Ultimate and Guts Championships (WUGC), held July 7–14 in Sakai, Japan (Osaka Prefecture). She wore #9 on the 24-player roster, alongside captains Danielle Fortin, Kate Werry, and Kristin Laurin.

A note on the sport: Ultimate Frisbee is a team field sport — running, passing a disc, scoring in an end zone — completely separate from disc golf, which is an individual course sport. Davison has no involvement in disc golf whatsoever.

"There really is no other sport as welcoming as ultimate. Before tournaments even begin, you'll see teens from across the Interior — from internationally recognized athletes to kids who are brand new to the game — warming up together, throwing together, and playing pickup games. Then when competition starts, they compete hard, but that respect and community never disappears. The future of our sport is incredibly bright."

Coaching & Community

As athletics coordinator and teacher at École Kelowna Secondary School, Davison oversees multiple sports including ultimate frisbee, volleyball, and wrestling. She helped introduce Ultimate as a sanctioned high school sport in Kelowna starting in 2019, and the KSS Owls have been highly competitive under her guidance.

She co-organizes Okanagan Youth Ultimate (OYU) and the annual 4v4 Khaos Ultimate Tournament, now in its fourth year. The February 2026 edition in Vernon drew nearly 60 youth athletes from Vernon, Kelowna, Kamloops, and Grand Forks across nine teams.

"Any time there's an opportunity for a student to be involved in u-sports there's so many benefits. From mental health to physical health to social and emotional health. And this is one of the most inexpensive sports you can play in high school. It's such a wonderful community at the high school level and beyond."

Advocacy & Personal Story

Just before helping the Spartans win their historic first national championship, Davison received an email informing her she was under investigation for having a relationship with another woman — a violation of TWU's "community covenant agreement." She lost her scholarship, was placed on behavioural probation, and was temporarily barred from the pitch.

"I was allowed back on the team, but it was a much different experience this time around. I felt like I was being watched. I can't remember the early days of being there and feeling like I was just a normal, regular part of the team."

She later advocated publicly for change, writing in TWU's student newspaper Mars' Hill and calling on the university to revise its covenant:

"If they're going to claim to be a diverse university… then let's work to make the campus more inclusive."

Her story was reported by Bethany Lindsay in The Province (November 2016). In June 2018, the Supreme Court of Canada ruled the mandatory covenant created inequitable barriers; by August 2018, TWU made the covenant non-mandatory for students.

"One of the most difficult things I faced when coming to Trinity Western was that I didn't know myself, and I didn't know that I didn't know myself. I thought I had things figured out, but various challenges — mentally, physically, and spiritually — have brought me closer to a more accurate perception of who I am as a person."
